# Surfacing Your Values

**An exercise for Black queer organisations**
*Part 1 of 2 — Commons Kit, BLKOUT UK*

---

## Why this exercise

Most values statements are useless to a decision-maker. *"We value community, integrity, and inclusion"* can't tell you whether to adopt a tool, take a funder, or decline a partnership. It's a virtue list, not a working document.

The mechanism that produces something useful is **negative space**. The questions below don't ask what you value. They ask what you've **refused**, where you've felt **friction**, and what you would **lose** before you'd compromise. Refusals reveal values that virtues hide.

You'll come out with two things:

1. **A values document** — five to seven specific operational lines you can hold a decision against.
2. **An indication of what your AI strategy has to consider** — not a strategy itself, a mirror of what your own answers say it can't ignore.

The values document is the input for Part 2 (the council kit). The AI-strategy indication is useful on its own, even if you never use Part 2.

---

## What you'll need

- About 90 minutes
- Two to four people who hold the decisions in your org (not the people who write the strategy decks — the people who say yes and no)
- Honest answers about what you've actually refused. If you haven't refused anything, this exercise is the wrong starting point — work out why first.

---

## The five questions

Work through them in order. Each question has a follow-up; the follow-up is where the operational content lives.

**1. Name three tools, platforms, or partnerships you've said no to in the last two years. Why?**
What did the refusal cost you? What did saying no protect?

**2. What would a funder offer that you'd turn down — and at what amount?**
Be specific about the offer and the number. If you can't name a number, the line isn't real yet.

**3. Name an organisation similar to yours that you'd never want to be mistaken for. What's the difference?**
Not what they do wrong — what they do that you're choosing not to do, and why.

**4. Whose work are you continuing? What would they not let you compromise on?**
Name the people (living or dead) whose work is the accountability you answer to. What's the specific commitment that comes from sitting in that lineage?

**5. Where do you and your closest collaborator most often disagree about strategy? What's the disagreement actually about?**
Beneath the surface argument is usually a values disagreement that hasn't been named. Name it.

---

## Writing it up

Convert your answers into five to seven lines. Each line should be **an operational rule**, not a virtue.

A virtue: *We value data sovereignty.*
An operational rule: *We do not adopt platforms that aggregate member data for commercial resale, and we do not host member contact data in jurisdictions where subject-access rights are weaker than UK GDPR.*

If a reader can't tell from your line whether a specific platform passes or fails, rewrite it until they can.

---

## Second output: what your answers say about AI strategy

Read your five-to-seven lines through this filter. You'll find your strategy already has to address:

- **Data sovereignty** — your refusals will cluster around where member data goes, who holds it, who can resell or model on it. Any tool that breaches that cluster fails before it starts.
- **Stack disposition** — your yes/no patterns on hosting, open-source, walled-garden SaaS reveal a stance you may not have written down. The boundary between what you adopt and what you refuse is your stack disposition. Write it.
- **Risk register** — the things you said you wouldn't trade are the test every strategy decision gets measured against. Three to five lines.
- **Accountability anchors** — the people you named in question 4 are the test, not the funder. Name them in the strategy doc.
- **Open questions** — the disagreement in question 5 is a tension the strategy will have to hold, not resolve prematurely. Naming it is the strategy work; pretending it's settled is the failure mode.

This isn't a recommendation. It's a mirror — your answers say your strategy has to consider these. What you do with that is yours.

---

## What this exercise is not

It is not a consultancy framework. It is not a way to brand your values. It is not a workshop deck for an away-day.

It is uncomfortable on purpose. It asks you to name what you've refused, which most organisations don't have language for. If the discomfort isn't there, the questions aren't doing their work.

## Lineage and use

This exercise emerged from BLKOUT's work on the digital commons for Black queer organisations in the UK. It is offered to peer organisations as part of a two-part kit. Free to use, adapt, remix, translate. Tell us what you find at commons@blkoutuk.com.

The exercise carries forward methods from earlier organisers and writers who insisted that values are visible in what we refuse, not what we proclaim. Specific debts: Audre Lorde on silence as expenditure; Joseph Beam on home as a question of who is in the room; the cooperative tradition of writing down what the membership won't do.

When you've completed Part 1, Part 2 is the **Dreamcatcher Council Kit** — a suite of historical judges and a meta-prompt that runs your values doc against any specific tool, partnership, or platform you're considering.
