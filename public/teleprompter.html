Open · 30 sec

I want to start

by naming the room.

Most of us in this conversation

share a lineage.

We are not strangers to each other's work,

even when we have not met.

The names we hold in common —

Lorde, Beam, Hemphill, Marsha,

Cummings, Alcock —

they are not slogans.

They are how we know who we are

when the institutions around us forget.

So before I tell you about a new BLKOUT,

I want to acknowledge

that whatever is new

is built from what we already share.

Beat 1 · What's structurally new · 90 sec

BLKOUT is now a Community Benefit Society

with an asset lock.

That's a legal form.

What it means in plain English

is that the things this organisation owns —

the platform, the relationships,

the data, the infrastructure —

cannot be sold off,

can't be turned into private equity,

can't be extracted from the community.

They have to stay in trust

for the community we exist to serve.

It sounds technical.

It is not technical.

It is a structural answer

to a question we keep being asked to forget:

who does this actually belong to.

Take house music.

It started in Black queer basements in Chicago,

in clubs we built

because no one else would have us.

We made it.

The mainstream took it —

turned it into Ibiza,

into a dance-music industry

that no longer remembers

whose hands first cut the records.

We kept making.

The lesson is in the pattern:

don't rely on the mainstream.

Be an owner, not a customer.

Particularly when your concerns and theirs

do not always align.

The CBS is what it looks like

when you take that lesson seriously

and write it into the deed.

Audre Lorde:

the master's tools

will never dismantle the master's house.

● ● ●
Beat 2 · The cooperative shift · 2 min

The second thing that's new

is what we now think BLKOUT is for.

For a long time,

BLKOUT was a platform.

A magazine, a content engine,

a broadcast voice.

That work mattered.

It also assumed we could speak for,

or at, our community

from a single position.

The honest reading after ten years is:

that's not what most of you need.

What most of you need

is each other.

So the shift is from broadcast

to cooperative infrastructure.

Not BLKOUT as the loudest voice in the room —

BLKOUT as one of the rooms.

What that looks like in practice

is at commons.blkoutuk.com.

The argument is two things.

Community control.

Corporate platforms extract our data,

throttle our reach,

and decide quarterly

whether our communities are worth keeping.

Owning the digital space we organise in

is the difference between

hosting a conversation

and renting permission to have one.

When the platforms tell us who they are —

through what they monetise,

who they ban, what they de-rank —

we should believe them.

Shared resources.

Each of our organisations is small.

The tools we need are not.

Building them alone

burns the time the mission needs.

Pooling what we build

leaves more capacity for the work —

and the things we make together

belong to all of us.

That's the proposition.

The operationalisation is a kit,

also at the commons site.

Two parts.

A values-surfacing exercise

that produces an operational values document

for your org —

not virtues, refusals.

And a council of six historical judges —

Lorde, Beam, Marsha,

Cummings, Alcock, Fashanu —

that you can run any platform,

partnership or tool against.

The council does not decide.

You do.

The kit just makes sure

the question gets asked

in the voices of people

who held the line in their own time.

Audre Lorde's line is the floor:

without community

there is no liberation.

The kit is what we have built

so that the line

is not just inspiration.

Beat 3 · The cultural reset · 90 sec

The third change

is in our cultural programme,

and it is the one I want to be

most honest about.

For a while,

the curation defaulted to celebration.

Flamboyance, brightness, joy —

the easy register.

That is real,

and it is half the truth.

The other half is grief, mourning, anger,

the AIDS-era losses,

the people we keep losing now.

A programme that only speaks

the upbeat language

is a programme that is lying

about Black queer life.

So we are deliberately

engaging the difficult.

Tongues Untied

alongside Paris Is Burning.

Hemphill's grief

alongside the dancefloor.

And while we are being honest:

BLKOUT exists for Black queer men in the UK.

Plural.

Across generations,

across diasporas,

across the parts of our community

that get easily disappeared

inside that single phrase.

We are not narrowing

to the comfortable subset.

We are not pretending

the rest aren't here.

Close · 30 sec

So that is what is new.

A structure that cannot sell us out.

A shift from broadcasting to cooperating.

A cultural register that holds

the shade alongside the light.

We have been navigating this kind of moment

together for decades.

Reactively, mostly.

The proposal is to do it

on purpose this time.

The kit is free,

the conversation is open,

and the question we are sitting with

is whether we could be stronger together this time —

not as a slogan,

as a working practice.

Without community

there is no liberation.

Lorde insisted on what that requires:

we

"nurture creative parts of each other

without always understanding

what will be created."

We do not know what you'll build with this.

That's the point.

Thank you.
